## About This Song
"Let's Dance" is the title track of David Bowie's 1983 album, produced with Nile Rodgers and featuring a then-unknown Stevie Ray Vaughan on guitar. It marked a deliberate pivot toward a polished, radio-ready pop and dance sound, a sharp turn from the art-rock era that preceded it, and became Bowie's biggest commercial hit, topping charts on both sides of the Atlantic.

## First Impressions
It's impossible not to move to this one; the groove locks in immediately and never lets go.

## My Reaction
This is Bowie's best version of his "I'm a top 40 songwriter" persona. In interviews and performances from the era, he came across as genuinely open, honest, and like he was just having fun with it. For all the flak this stretch of his career gets, I'm glad it's how I found him. Digging back into it later, even fans from the time loved it; the backlash came afterward, once the follow-up albums landed and didn't hold up nearly as well. Even critics who wrote it off as sellout radio fare tended to admit something like: if anyone could do this without losing their cool points, it was Bowie, and good for him. "Modern Love" in particular is still an absolute banger. It was all good times back then, to the point where even Frank Zappa took his shots at it, and it never landed as anything worse than affectionate ribbing.
